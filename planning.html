Planning Your Park Project

Successful parks start with thoughtful planning. Learn how to assess community needs, engage stakeholders, and develop a comprehensive park plan.

Getting Started

The planning phase is critical to creating a park that truly serves your community. Start by understanding what your residents need and want from their public spaces.

  • Community Assessment: Survey residents to understand demographics, needs, and preferences for park amenities and programming
  • Site Analysis: Evaluate potential locations considering accessibility, environmental factors, and existing infrastructure
  • Stakeholder Engagement: Involve town officials, residents, local organizations, and neighboring landowners in the planning process
  • Vision Development: Create a shared vision that reflects community values and long-term goals

Key Planning Steps

  • Form a Planning Committee: Assemble a diverse group of community members, municipal staff, and subject matter experts
  • Conduct Community Meetings: Host public forums, focus groups, and workshops to gather input and build support
  • Inventory Existing Parks: Document current park facilities, usage patterns, and maintenance needs
  • Identify Gaps and Opportunities: Determine what types of facilities and programs are lacking in your community
  • Set Priorities: Rank projects based on community need, feasibility, and available resources
  • Develop a Master Plan: Create a comprehensive document that guides park development over 5-10 years
  • Establish Implementation Timeline: Break the master plan into phases with realistic timeframes and milestones

Vermont-Specific Considerations

  • Plan for year-round use considering Vermont's distinct seasons and winter recreation opportunities
  • Consider Vermont's Act 250 land use regulations for larger projects
  • Engage with regional planning commissions and local conservation groups
  • Incorporate Vermont's rural character and natural landscape into your planning
  • Plan for stormwater management and climate resilience
  • Consider connections to existing trail networks and green spaces
