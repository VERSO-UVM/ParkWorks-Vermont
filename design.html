Designing Inclusive, Functional Parks

Great park design balances aesthetics, functionality, accessibility, and environmental stewardship. Explore best practices for creating spaces that serve all community members.

Design Principles

  • Universal Accessibility: Ensure parks are accessible to people of all ages and abilities, following ADA guidelines
  • Safety First: Design with clear sightlines, appropriate lighting, and safe circulation patterns
  • Flexibility: Create multi-use spaces that can accommodate diverse activities and programming
  • Natural Integration: Work with existing topography, vegetation, and natural features
  • Community Identity: Reflect local character, history, and Vermont's unique sense of place
  • Sustainability: Use environmentally friendly materials and design for long-term durability

Essential Park Elements

  • Play Areas: Age-appropriate play equipment for toddlers, children, and tweens
  • Open Spaces: Flexible lawn areas for sports, picnics, and informal recreation
  • Pathways: Accessible walking and biking paths connecting park features
  • Seating: Benches and gathering areas for rest and socialization
  • Shade Structures: Trees, pavilions, or shelters for sun protection
  • Restrooms: Clean, accessible facilities appropriately located
  • Parking: Adequate parking with accessible spaces and bike racks
  • Signage: Wayfinding, rules, and interpretive information

Designing for Vermont's Climate

  • Select hardy, native plant species that thrive in Vermont's climate zones (3-5)
  • Plan for snow storage and winter maintenance access
  • Design drainage systems to handle spring snowmelt and heavy rains
  • Include winter recreation features like sledding hills, ice skating areas, or snowshoe trails
  • Choose materials that withstand freeze-thaw cycles
  • Consider seasonal aesthetics and year-round visual interest

Inclusive Design Guidelines

  • Provide accessible routes throughout the park with appropriate slopes and surfaces
  • Include sensory play elements and equipment for children with disabilities
  • Design spaces that accommodate multigenerational activities
  • Consider cultural inclusivity in programming and design
  • Provide quiet spaces alongside active areas
  • Ensure adequate lighting for evening use and safety

Working with Design Professionals

  • Landscape architects can create detailed site plans and specifications
  • Civil engineers address grading, drainage, and utilities
  • Consider hiring Vermont-based professionals familiar with local conditions
  • Request portfolios of similar park projects
  • Engage designers early in the planning process
